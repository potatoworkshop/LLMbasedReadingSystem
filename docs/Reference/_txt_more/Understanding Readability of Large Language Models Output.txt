Understanding Readability of Large Language Models Output:
An Empirical Analysis
Fiammetta Marullia,∗, Lelio Campanile a, Maria Stella de Biase a, Stefano Marrone a, Laura
Verdea, Marianna Bifulco b
aUniversit` a della Campania “Luigi Vanvitelli”- Dept. of Maths and Physics, Caserta, Italy
bIndependent ICT Specialist, Napoli, Italy
Abstract
Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have seen some impressive leaps, achieving the ability to accomplish several tasks,
from text completion to powerful chatbots. The great variety of available LLMs and the fast pace of technological innovations in
this field, is making LLM assessment a hard task to accomplish: understanding not only what such a kind of systems generate but
also which is the quality of their results is of a paramount importance. Generally, the quality of a synthetically generated object
could refer to the reliability of the content, to the lexical variety or coherence of the text. Regarding the quality of text generation,
an aspect that up to now has not been adequately discussed is concerning the readability of textual artefacts. This work focuses
on the latter aspect, proposing a set of experiments aiming to better understanding and evaluating the degree of readability of texts
automatically generated by an LLM. The analysis is performed through an empirical study based on: considering a subset of five
pre-trained LLMs; considering a pool of English text generation tasks, with increasing difficulty, assigned to each of the models;
and, computing a set of the most popular readability indexes available from the computational linguistics literature. Readability
indexes will be computed for each model to provide a first perspective of the readability of textual contents artificially generated
can vary among different models and under different requirements of the users. The results obtained by evaluating and comparing
different models provide interesting insights, especially into the responsible use of these tools by both beginners and not overly
experienced practitioners.

1. Introduction
Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs), have thrust generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) mod-
els into the spotlight for a wider audience. While Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [1] and Variational Au-
toencoders (V AEs)[2] have existed for some time within the research community, LLMs have provided compelling
evidence of their potential to a broader user base. Among the large variety of objects that LLMs can provide by their
generation mechanisms, Natural Language Processing (NLP) is almost certainly the most impacted field, e.g., ranging
from automated content generation and machine translation to an empowered information retrieval and to sentiment
analysis. These models, trained on vast amounts of text data, possess remarkable capabilities in producing human-like
text content. However, amid the excitement surrounding their capabilities, a critical concern emerges: how to evaluate
and assess the quality of artificial objects automatically, without the human expertise in the loop.
More generally, evaluating the quality of textual contents is an old problem rooted in linguistic theory; a subset of
the most adopted dimensions for the quality analysis of textual objects are: (1) grammar and mechanics(including
both grammatical errors checks and readability scores), (2) vocabulary and style(covering lexical diversity, sentence
length variation, etc.), and (3) coherence and cohesion(dealing with co-reference resolution and logical flow analy-
sis). Hence, evaluating automatically generated contents can offer not trivial challenges too. So far, the most attention
of the research has been addressed on what is possible to generate by such kinds of systems and to improve the results
of the process generation. However, the concept itself of “improvement” and how it could be evaluated has not been
clearly stated yet in a systematical framework: in fact, very little prominence has been paid to this latter aspect. First,
it should be clarified: (1) what we mean for the quality of an artificially generated text, (2) how can we measure it
(ordinal and/or quantitative scales), and (3) what kind of measures we should perform to estimate it. Providing the
most suitable quality framework is affected by the kind of artifact: some of the affecting factors are — but not limited
to — reliability of the content (both for the textual and visual contents [3, 4]), the readability, the lexical variety, and
the text coherence.
Indeed, while LLMs excels in generating coherent and contextually relevant texts, they often fall short in producing
truly readable and understandable contents. This discrepancy between the fluency of generated text and its actual
readability poses significant challenges across multiple domains, including content creation, education, journalism,
and user interfaces. Anyway, the readability of textual artefacts represents an aspect that up to now has not been
adequately discussed. Analysing and understanding the factors contributing to the readability issues of LLM-generated
text becomes imperative. Factors such as syntactic complexity, semantic coherence, and stylistic inconsistency play
crucial roles in determining the readability of generated content. Moreover, the lack of control over the tone, style,
and vocabulary of generated text further exacerbates the readability challenges, especially in applications where clarity
and coherence are paramount.
It should also not be undervalued that the readability problems associated with LLM-generated texts have far-
reaching implications. In educational settings, poorly readable content may hinder comprehension and learning out-
comes, impacting students’ academic performance. In journalistic and content creation domains, readability directly
influences audience engagement and retention. Additionally, in user interface design and chatbot interactions, read-
ability determines the effectiveness of communication and user experience. Addressing the problems of readability
in LLM-generated text requires a multidisciplinary approach combining insights from linguistics, cognitive science,
and computer science. Developing techniques to enhance readability while preserving the fluency and coherence of
generated text is essential for realising the full potential of LLMs in diverse applications.
In this work, we delve into the intricacies of readability challenges associated with LLM-generated text. We ex-
plore the underlying factors contributing to these challenges and examine existing approaches aimed at improving
readability. Through empirical analysis and case studies, we aim to provide insights useful to understand mechanisms
to implement in LLMs for mitigating readability issues in LLM-generated content, thereby advancing the usability
and effectiveness of these powerful language models in real-world applications.
The paper is structured as follows: Section 2 reviews the main papers of the scientific literature and also recalls
useful background information; Section 3 describes the methodology followed in the work; Section 4 describes the
setting and the di fferent experiments run in this work, along with a preliminary discussion about the results of the
experimental campaign, extracting some lesson learned; Section 5 ends the paper and sketches some future research
directions.

2. Background and Related Work
At the best of our knowledge, there are currently very few research works relate to the objective of the present
paper. The study described in [5] evaluated the impact of the contents generated by LLMs in the field of education to
explore the potential of a precise system, namely the OpenAI Chat GPT3.5[6] to aid student learning. It explores the
opportunity to tailor the LLMs to produce high-quality contents, where the quality is defined as highly personalized
reading comprehension exercises for middle school English learners in China. In [7], a study that explores a readability
assessment and a text simplification for generating science documents targeting a multilingual educational context was
proposed. Different types of characterisation methods related to text classification and simplification, following a set
of pedagogical approaches, were evaluated. Even if it is precisely focused on three languages (Basque, Spanish and
English), it suggests an extendable methodology for teachers to follow so that they can adjust and assess materials
according to the student profiles in the classroom. The work of [8] represents the closest paper to our study: it focuses
on approaches for generating educational materials with different levels of readability by using LLMs. It assessed the
capability of some LLMs, to generate content at various readability levels through zero-shot and few-shot prompting.
Considered LLMs are: GPT-3.5[6], LLaMA-2 70B[9], and Mixtral 8x7B[10]. Different levels of readability have been
obtained by adopting text simplification and transformation rules; while increasing the readability of a document by
the means of a smaller set of words and a simplified vocabulary, the context preservation is a fundamental aspect to
consider. This latter aspect is among the insights of the study proposed in this work, where we aimed to investigate
according to which criteria different LLMs would take a decision to cut contents and simplify complex linguistic
structures into other simpler ones.
To this aim, several well assessed text quality evaluation indexes are available from the literature: a subset of them
can be adopted to assess various aspects of text, from readability and fluency to coherence and informativeness.
With the advent and advances in AI, automatic text generation tools, such as the ones based on the LLM, have
provided another source of artificial but realistic content. Among these, the textual documents can space from narrative
texts to argumentative ones, including descriptive and creative ones, such as poems and songs, short stories and fables,
technical reports and dissertations.
Naturally, the problem of determining the degree to which the artificial text is “correct” is a challenge that cannot be
solved in a bit. First, as it also happens when a human-crafted text is evaluated, the meaning of “correctness” for a text
should be clearly stated: this feature can refer to the syntactical correctness of the text, or it may refer to the content of
the text that is matching the topic that was requested, or both. Beyond the correctness, “completeness” and the “lexical
richness” are other two typical features for stating the overall quality of a manuscript. Evaluating the readability of
text generated by language models is a complex problem, implying a trade-off between human (subjective) judgments
and automated (objective) metrics.
Dealing with the problem requires a multifaceted approach able to account for the various dimensions of readabil-
ity; hence, it can be summarised in:
• Lack of Consensus on Evaluation Methods: there is no one-size-fits-all approach to assess how good the model’s
text is. It is, usually, compared to a human-written example, but this benchmark can be subjective.
• Metrics do not Capture Readability Nuances: common metrics, like perplexity, measure how likely a sentence
is from the model’s training data, not how easy it is for a human to understand.
• Subjectivity: readability is inherently subjective and varies based on the reader’s background, language profi-
ciency, and familiarity with the topic. What one reader finds easy to read, another might find difficult
• Existing Metrics: traditional metrics, such as the Flesch-Kincaid readability test, are often based on surface-
level text features like sentence length and word complexity. These metrics may not fully capture the nuances
of readability in the context of automatically generated text, including coherence, logical flow, and contextual
relevance.
• Context and Cohesion: automatically generated text can sometimes be syntactically correct but semantically
incoherent. Evaluating readability requires assessing whether the text makes sense in a broader context, which
is difficult to quantify with existing automated tools.
